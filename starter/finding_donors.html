Metrics and the Naive Predictor¶

CharityML, equipped with their research, knows individuals that make more than $50,000 are most likely to donate to their charity. Because of this, *CharityML* is particularly interested in predicting who makes more than $50,000 accurately. It would seem that using accuracy as a metric for evaluating a particular model's performace would be appropriate. Additionally, identifying someone that does not make more than $50,000 as someone who does would be detrimental to *CharityML*, since they are looking to find individuals willing to donate. Therefore, a model's ability to precisely predict those that make more than $50,000 is more important than the model's ability to recall those individuals. We can use F-beta score as a metric that considers both precision and recall:

$$ F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{\text{precision} \cdot \text{recall}}{\left( \beta^2 \cdot \text{precision} \right) + \text{recall}} $$

In particular, when $\beta = 0.5$, more emphasis is placed on precision. This is called the $F_{0.5}$ score (or F-score for simplicity).

Looking at the distribution of classes (those who make at most $50,000, and those who make more), it's clear most individuals do not make more than $50,000. This can greatly affect accuracy, since we could simply say "this person does not make more than $50,000" and generally be right, without ever looking at the data! Making such a statement would be called naive, since we have not considered any information to substantiate the claim. It is always important to consider the naive prediction for your data, to help establish a benchmark for whether a model is performing well. That been said, using that prediction would be pointless: If we predicted all people made less than $50,000, CharityML would identify no one as donors.

Note: Recap of accuracy, precision, recall¶

Accuracy measures how often the classifier makes the correct prediction. Its the ratio of the number of correct predictions to the total number of predictions (the number of test data points).

Precision tells us what proportion of messages we classified as spam, actually were spam. It is a ratio of true positives (words classified as spam, and which are actually spam) to all positives (all words classified as spam, irrespective of whether that was the correct classification), in other words it is the ratio of

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{\text{True Positives}} {\text{True Positives} + \text{False Positives}}$$

Recall (sensitivity) tells us what proportion of messages that actually were spam were classified by us as spam. It is a ratio of true positives (words classified as spam, and which are actually spam) to all the words that were actually spam, in other words it is the ratio of

$$\text{Recall} = \frac{\text{True Positives}} {\text{True Positives} + \text{False Negatives}}$$

For classification problems that are skewed in their classification distributions, like in our case, for example, if we had 100 text messages and only 2 were spam and the remaining 98 weren't, accuracy is not a very good metric. We could classify 90 messages as not spam (including the 2 that were spam, but we classify them as not spam, hence they would be false negatives) and 10 as spam (all 10 false positives) and still get a reasonably good accuracy score. For such cases, precision and recall come in very handy. These two metrics can be combined to get the F1 score and the weighted average (harmonic mean) of the precision and recall scores. This score can range from 0 to 1, with 1 being the best possible F1 score(we take the harmonic mean when dealing with ratios).

Gradient Boosting¶

Real-world application: Gradient boosting models are important for supporting investment decisions, such as portfolio optimization, and predicting stock price movements or credit risks based on historical data.

Strengths:

  • It often achieves higher predictive accuracy than random forests.
  • It sequentially builds trees, with each new tree minimizing the errors (residuals) of the previous ones, effectively creating a strong learner from an ensemble of weak learners.
  • Techniques like regularization and early stopping help to prevent overfitting.

Weaknesses:

  • It is more sensitive to noisy data and outliers compared to random forests.
  • It can be more prone to overfitting if hyperparameters are not tuned carefully.
  • Training can be slower, particularly on very large datasets, because trees are built sequentially, not in parallel.

Suitability for census data:

Gradient boosting is well-suited for census data because it can capture subtle patterns and achieves top performance in many complex machine learning competitions. Given the potentially complex relationships within demographic data, gradient boosting may have better accuracy if the data is clean and sufficient time is spent on tuning.

Logistic Regression¶

Real-world application: In healthcare, logistic regression is a staple for clinical risk prediction. For example, researchers use it to predict the likelihood of patients developing a certain disease (like diabetes) based on lab tests and medical history.

Strengths:

  • It is easy to implement, interpret, and train efficiently.
  • It outputs well-calibrated probabilities along with classification results.
  • It performs well when the dataset's features are linearly separable.

Weaknesses:

  • It assumes linearity between the independent variables.
  • It struggles with non-linear problems and fails to capture complex relationships naturally.
  • Its performance can degrade if important non-relevant features are included.
  • It can underperform more complex algorithms like random forests on data with complex, non-linear boundaries.

Suitability for census data:

It is a good candidate when interpretability is a primary goal, allowing researchers to easily understand which demographic factors are the strongest predictors of an outcome. Answer: Gradient Boosting works by building a powerful, highly accurate "super-learner" out of a large collection of simple, weak learners (basic decision trees). The key is the sequential training process. The model starts with a simple initial guess, and then trains subsequent weak advisors. It identifies where the team of advisors is still missing the mark and trains the next tree to step in and correct that specific error, similar to how one might refine several drafts of a research paper. This iterative process allows the model to incrementally and efficiently reduce its overall errors until it achieves a high level of accuracy.

When it is time to predict if a new individual is likely to donate, all the simple decision trees that were trained in sequence work together. The final output is the cumulative sum of every single tree's tiny, careful adjustments, resulting in a prediction about the potential donor's likelihood of contributing.

import numpy as np

def find_best_threshold(y_true, y_proba, beta=0.5):
    best_t = 0
    best_f = 0
    for t in np.arange(0.0, 1.0, 0.01):
        preds = (y_proba >= t).astype(int)
        f = fbeta_score(y_true, preds, beta=beta)
        if f > best_f:
            best_f = f
            best_t = t
    return best_t, best_f
